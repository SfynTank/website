Find out more

SFYN Tank is an experimental think-tank to our greater Slow Food mechanism; an idea-generation and solution-design based event to influence which social initiatives Slow Food can spearhead within the context of our global food movement.

With the input of interaction designers, product designers, and food designers, alongside the gastronomic expertise of our Slow Food Youth Network delegates and members, we can innovate solutions to social food problems. Come attend our full-day design workshop on September 24th, 2016 to employ a multidisciplinary approach that encompasses studying, storytelling, and thinking with a critical design eye to solve nine social problems our food system faces–from shifting our idea of meat consumption to considering how to integrate the food culture of migrants. Through open-exchange of expertise, out-of-the-box thinking, and a design approach, we can make meaningful impact on our global food system.

Repurposing abandoned spaces for food production

Our world is well developed. Over-developed, in fact. What happens to an old subway line that goes out of service? Or to former factories, dilapidated homes, city lots, and decaying farmhouses? Can food communities re-imagine and utilize such spaces for food production to avoid clearing new land?

Bridging the urban rural gap

Contemporary city-life often means a lack of connection to nature, and specifically to our food. Convenience reins the life of a city-dweller, and a relationship with natural processes is lost. How can we maintain our relationship with the earth and her fruit in a concrete jungle?

Diplomatizing food-communication

from documentary television series food travel, to chef’s instagram, to foodie web-magazines, food-communication is trendy. But the it’s the developed world that leads in this effort, and when we see communication about food cultures of the developing world, it can often be exploitative. How can we diplomatize global food communication? How can remote communities be in control of their food stories?

When does innovative design preserve indigenous knowledge

contemporary innovation implies the digital, and indigenous life implies the analog. But many indigenous agricultural practices, food processes, and cooking methods were once innovative. How can digital innovation act to restore, improve, and preserve traditional food knowledge?

Designing sustainable kitchens

from the home-kitchen, to professional kitchens, to mobile kitchens. The experience of chefs and cooks in our SFYN network who prioritize waste-reduction and honoring ingredients can start to mobilize with designers in dictating how to redesign the kitchen for restaurants, schools, hospitals, or the home in order to have tools, methods, and space arrangement that enhances the sustainability of where food is transformed.

Thinking outside the “can”

Slow Fish, the community within Slow Food that focus on sustainable fishery practices, believes there’s a lot of dignity in preserved fish. In a world that demands the freshest fish in every corner of the earth, preserved fish has gotten a bad rep. Whereas fresh tuna, salmon, and cod can be found year-round in the most unlikely of places, canned-fish is often considered poor quality, cheap, and met with suspicion. The demand for fresh fish like tuna and salmon are at the root of overfishing, farmed-fish, and multiple environmental, economic, and social hazards, when in fact canned-fish is a traditional waste-reduction and frugal-eating cultural habit for many seaside communities worldwide. How can we re-dignify preserved fish and think outside of the can?

Improving access to the slaughterhouse for livestock-farmers

Slow Food’s initiative on meat-production and consumption, Slow Meat, represents many livestock farmers, and a common problem many face worldwide is easy, affordable access to a hygienic, certified slaughterhouse. In a campaign to mobilize the slaughterhouse, how can design improve on the mobility and service of a slaughterhouse while honoring the work of a farmer and the lives of his animals.
